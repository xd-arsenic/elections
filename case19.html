Section 40 Cascade

Case 19: The Vote That Flipped

The Commission had told candidates Section 40 only applied to RSOs — a limit they invented, not one in the code. SJP is not an RSO. Miranda was tried first and acquitted under that rule. Simpson was next — also 4–3 not guilty. A motion to reconsider flipped Simpson's verdict to guilty. After that, every candidate was convicted. Miranda kept his acquittal.

The Rule at Issue

Section 40 of the AS Election Code governs what student organizations may do during an election. The code text itself does not limit the term "student organization" to any particular registration status.

The Elections Commission, however, had invented such a limit. In trainings, in office hours, and in guidance given to candidates, the Commission told the campus that Section 40 applied only to Registered Student Organizations (RSOs) — the formally recognized campus groups that complete the RSO registration process through Student Life.

A Rule the Commission Made Up

The "RSO-only" reading is not in the Election Code. It was the Commission's own gloss — a limit it authored and then taught as if it were binding.

SJP (Students for Justice in Palestine) is not an RSO. Under the Commission's own invented rule, its voting guide — the document at the center of this entire case series — was outside the scope of Section 40.

That is the rule candidates were told to rely on. It is the rule the Commission itself applied when it first voted on Simpson's case. And it is the rule the Commission silently abandoned the moment it produced an inconvenient result.

1. Miranda — Tried First, Acquitted

The first Section 40 charge from the SJP voting guide was filed against Ricardo Miranda. The Commission applied its own RSO-only rule. SJP is not an RSO, therefore Section 40 did not reach Miranda's appearance on the guide.

Miranda's Verdict

NOT GUILTY. The commissioners who would later flip on Simpson voted to acquit Miranda cleanly under the RSO-only rule.

No motion to reconsider was introduced. Miranda's acquittal stood.

2. Simpson — Next Up

Simpson's case came next. Same code section. Same evidence source. Same SJP voting guide. The Commission voted.

Simpson's Initial Vote

4–3 — NOT GUILTY.

The hearing moved on. The next case was about to begin.

The Motion Nobody Saw Coming

Out of nowhere, someone moved to reconsider — not on Miranda's case, not on any earlier case, but on Simpson's, which had just been decided. No new evidence was presented. No legal argument had been overlooked. Nothing in the record had changed in the minutes since the 4–3 vote.

The motion itself was the event. A verdict that had already been handed down was suddenly back on the table, and the same seven commissioners — who had just applied the RSO-only rule twice in a row without issue — were asked to vote again.

This time the numbers came out differently. Simpson was found guilty. The rule had not changed. The facts had not changed. SJP was still not an RSO. The only thing that had changed was that somebody decided the previous answer was no longer acceptable.

And Miranda's acquittal — decided under the exact same standard, minutes earlier — was not touched.

Same evidence. Same code. Same commissioners. Miranda: not guilty (stands). Simpson: not guilty, then reconsidered, then guilty. — Case 19 record, reconstructed from the hearing

The Cascade

The reconsidered verdict in Simpson's case became the template for everything that followed — while Miranda's clean acquittal, decided under the same rule just minutes earlier, was never revisited. Every subsequent Section 40 charge arising from the SJP voting guide — against every other candidate who appeared on it — was resolved as guilty.

Pattern After Simpson
  • 1. Miranda — tried first. Not guilty. No reconsideration. Acquittal stands.
  • 2. Simpson — tried second. Not guilty (4–3), then reconsidered and flipped to guilty.
  • 3+ Every candidate after Simpson — charged under Section 40 for the same voting guide. Every single one found guilty.

Miranda is the only person who was ever acquitted under Section 40 in this cycle. Everyone else on the same guide, under the same rule, was convicted.

Why This Case Matters

Rule by Reconsideration

A motion to reconsider is supposed to correct error — to revisit a ruling in light of something the body overlooked. Here, it was used to overwrite a ruling that had applied the Commission's own interpretation correctly. The "correction" was what introduced the error.

Inconsistency Isn't Neutral

If Section 40 applies to non-RSOs, then the Commission's previous guidance was wrong and countless past cases were decided under a false standard. If Section 40 applies only to RSOs — as the Commission has consistently said — then every conviction flowing from Simpson is void. There is no version of the rule under which the cascade of guilty verdicts is lawful.

The Pattern Repeats

Case 19 is the template for what happens across this election cycle: when an initial ruling favors an opposition candidate, the body finds a procedural lever — a motion to reconsider, a vague clause, a "clarification" — to reverse itself. See also Case 13 (identical evidence, opposite verdicts against Yelkovan and Miranda) and Case 24 (convicting on a clause the Board itself admitted was "unclear").

The Question Nobody Answered

The code was the same for everyone. The evidence was the same voting guide. The commissioners were the same people in the same room on the same day. Only one person walked out with an acquittal.

Why was Miranda the only candidate who didn't receive a guilty verdict under Section 40? — The question this case exists to ask

Nobody moved to reconsider Miranda. Nobody re-opened his verdict once the Commission decided that the RSO-only rule no longer mattered. The same body that flipped Simpson's acquittal and then convicted every candidate who followed left Miranda's acquittal untouched — even though it rested on a standard the Commission spent the rest of the day refusing to apply.
